the wedge

intergrated linear town as channel, link, & stimulator

location | Beijing, China
type | academic
role | 1st author of research; individual design
advisor | Feng Song of Peking University
date | Mar to Oct 2020
How effective was planning to guide urbanisation in the sprawling Beijing? While the built-up area is subjected to planning control, economic hot zones continued the slower and concentric profusion or followed other robust economic cores, highlighting the importance of economic basis in new town creation. Based on this conclusion, and inspired by a strand of prosperous towns stretching to the northwest driven by Zhongguancun Science Park, I propose a linear new town in the northeast thhat uses multi-modal transport to attain full integration with its base (another dynamic science park), and to receive "spill-overs." Meanwhile, its elongated shape and linear transport pattern guaranteed inside density, avoiding sprawl and acting as economic stimulator to a much larger area.

1. motivation
Beijing’s expansion is characteristic of concentric sprawl. Economic activities still converge in the center, to and from which commuting is onerou: 1 estimated at 52 minutes per day on average. 2 De-densified concentric sprawl is a detrimental pattern. 3 It undermines a city’s ecological base, and reduces accessibility to jobs and services for those living outside the center, deepening spatial inequality. Long commuting jams the traffic and adds to air pollution, too. At the same time, the planning authorities have been trying to plan a polycentric structure to compat this sprawl pattern. Therefore, this research aims to find the inconsistency between Beijing’s built-up area expansion and the spread of economic activities since 1978, and see how planning influenced either element in the projected centers.
2. history of planning
1 – Pre-modern: Political void & economic large ring agglomeration 4
2 – Plan of 1950s: Centralization 5
3 – 1960s: Anti-planning & de-densification 5
4 – 1980s & 1990s: Rapid swelling
Beijing’s current center was established in the 13th century—an annular central agglomeration larger than most ancient cities, as a result of circumventing the political imposition in the center. Since 1949, Beijing underwent seven rounds of planning, the 2016 Plan being the latest.
Economic growth, especially since the marketization of urban land in the 1990s accelerated urban sprawl.
3. planned satellites
1992, 2004, & 2016 policy projection:
the multi-centric Beijing
Two layers of “satellites”—the fringe clusters and the New Towns—were repeatedly proposed in the 1992, 2004 and 2016 Plans. 6  In particular, the 2016 Plan denoted Tongzhou as the Administrative Center to be above the other ‘centers’.
4. city expanded
Built-up area growth compared to planning
In built-up land control, planning has been effective since 2000.
Method: We extracted built-up areas using supervised classification8 7  of LandSat RS images from 1980 to 2019, which were divided into six types: i. urban built-up, ii. non-urban built-up, iii. farm, iv. water, v. forests. vi. other. To acquire smooth boundaries of the urban built-up area, we imposed a 30 m * 30 m grid, and calculated for each grid cell its local built-up density—percentage of the urban built-up type within 1 km radius. Urban built-up area is the agglomeration of all cells exceeding 60% in local built-up density.
Findings: 1. Before 2000, the sprawl of only of the central cluster dominated, with one exception of Jiuxianqiao in the northeast. The “fringe clusters” or “New Towns” as planned did not gain considerable development. 2. Since 2000, the development of relatively independent fringe clusters (New Towns) became evident. The partition between planned fringe clusters was remained, too.
5. activities also expanded
(but slower)
Un-match between growth of built-up area & economic hot zones
Compared to built-up land expansion, the spatial spread of economic activities follows a much more concentric path.
Method: We extracted built-up areas using supervised method. We used two sources to estimate the changes of each subdistrict’s relative economic importance over the years.
Source 1: Economic Census data of 2008, recording all economic units at survey time and their founding time, address, staff size, etc. Subdistrict A’s relative economic importance in year B is defined as the ratio of Subdistrict A’s economic scale in year B to the year B’s total; the ratio is approximated as the ratio of Subdistrict A’s remaining economic scale from year B until 2008 to year B’s total remaining until 2008, operationalized as the ratio of staff size of Subdistrict A’s economic units founded before year B to the total staff size founded before year B by 2008. 8
Source 2: POI’s from Baidu map, a major digital map provider in China, excluding pure geographic labels. The relative economic scale of Subdistrict A is the number of POI’s of Subdistrict A in relation to the total number of POI’s.
Findings: 1. Before 2000, economically activities were highly concentrated in core areas (even though they were underestimated), with the exception of Shijingshan (heavy industries) and the airport. 2. Since 2000, economic activities slowly perfused while adhering mostly to the center, producing the one-center-thin-edge situation. Many of the fringe clusters and satellite towns did not obtain any extra economic importance, unless they had very robust industrial basis (e.g. high-tech).
6. effectiveness of planned satellites
Winners and losers of the satellite centers
Have planning efforts to make new centers succeeded? Yes and no.
1. In terms of economic concentration, the central cluster did not weaken in domination. 2. Many fringe clusters or New Towns did not respond to planning. They either continue to be a small local center not growing in scale; or they never have been a local center from beginning to end. 3. The successful ones all have robust industrial or administrative basis: (a) Qinghe-Shahe-Changping, the northwest high-tech axis. Injected with high-tech industries supported by Zhongguancun, this axis developed into an economically strong “antenna” extending from the center. (b) Tongzhou, the new administrative center. The whole system of Beijing metropolitan government have been moved away to Tongzhou under a pressure-relieving plan, galvanizing its growth.
7. conclusions
The “dual expansion” pattern
After initial sprawl in the 1980s and 1990s, the 2004 planning was effective in built-up area control. However, planning was less successful in shifting economic concentration from cores to areas away from center. The economically active zones did extent, though following a one-center-thin-edges pattern, unless the prospect fringe cluster or New Town stands on robust industrial or administrative basis. The “dual expansion” : (1) Built-up area expansion is quicker, easier to attain, and more malleable to planning efforts. (2) Spread of economic vitality is slower, adheres to the existing center and follow market regularities. Those successfully made new centers all have robust economic basis.
The ‘dual expansion’ creates the innate tendency towards unhealthy concentric sprawl. High demand in construction triggers further built-up land growth, while economic activities’s spatial shift falls far behind. The result is a larger city with broad and thin edges.
