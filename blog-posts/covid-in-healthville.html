## WHAT IS THIS MODEL?
This model simulates the spread of an infectious virus in a simple town. The model can simulate residents' going to work, school, and other places while transmitting the virus, or staying at home when getting symptomatic, and becoming sick, recover, or die. By setting parameters to control the town's land use and demographics makeup, people's behavior, and the virus's characteristics, the model can simulate the spread of the virus and output the number of infected, recovered, and dead people in the process. [Play with the tool in Netlogo](../assets/projects/covid%20in%20Healthville/netlogo.html)
Agent-based models like this are suitable for a use case like this for the following reasons:

- Ability to capture the complexity of human behavior and interactions. Disease transmission is a complex process that involves many factors, such as demography, behavior, and virology. Compared to top-down models, agent-based models are able to capture these factors and simulate the interactions between them.
- Customizability for testing out different scenarios. Agent-based models help us understand or anticipate disease transmission scenarios under different demographical, behavioral, and virological settings. Such understanding may otherwise be difficult to obtain from real-life data.
Users should be cautioned that agent-based models are not guaranteed to be true representations of reality. They are only models, and they are only as good as the assumptions and parameters that go into them. However, two points may be made to address this concern:
- Agent-based models like this are highly adaptible. Once we have set up a framework like this one, we can adjust the model assumptions to fit the real-life situation.
- Agent-based models like this can also be validated against real-life data. After this validation, we are more confident in the validity of the model and better trust the simulation result for scenarios that deviate from the real-life situation.
## HOW THE MODEL WORKS
### The Town
The "town" is made of a 64x64 grid of patches or "cells", and each cell represents a land-use type. There are four land-use types:
- Schools. The town as a number of schools (controlled by the user) randomly placed within the town.
- Workplaces. Workplaces are where some residents go to work. The workplaces are more likely to be placed near *schools* and other *work centers* (the number of which controlled by the user).
- Homes. Homes are where residents live and the density of which is controlled by the user.
### Residents and Their Routines
Each household corresponds to a home. Within each household, the model assumes that there are three types of residents: students, workers, and non-workers, and they follow different daily routines. Users may control the sizes of the households in terms of three types of residents.
- Students go to school every day. After school, they may or may not go to another location close to their school or home.
- Workers go to work every day. After work, they may or may not go to another location to perform a side routine close to their work or home.
- Non-workers (primarily pensioners) do not go to school or work, but they may or may not go to some location to perform a side routine close to their homes.
Residents may choose to avoid their primary and side routines and stay at home because of the disease. There are three types of "personalities":
- **"Careless"** residents will go out and perform their routines normally unless they are *more than* mildly symptomatic.
- **"Cautious"** residents will not go out if any of their household members becomes symptomatic.
- The other residents will stay at home if they themselves are symptomatic, regardless of whether their household members are symptomatic.
### Infections
Infections happen whenever different residents are in the same cell during their routines and in-household. In public places, the likelihood of a susceptible resident to catch the disease is dependent on 1. the number of infected residents in the same cell, and 2. the ratio of infected residents to total residents, and 3. a factor set by the user. Specifically:
- Infection probability is exponential to the ratio of infected people, all else staying constant: `infection-ratio = (exp (multiplier * (sick-ratio - 1))) / (multiplier)`, where `sick-ratio` is the ratio of infected people at the same cell.
- In the above equation, `multiplier` is determined by the absolute number of infected people and the user-defined factor: `multiplier = 1.2 / (how-infectious * num-sick-people)`, where `num-sick-people` is the number of infected people, and `how-infectious` is the user-defined factor.
In-household transmission is more straightforward. If any household member becomes infected, the probability of their household members getting infected is directly controlled by the user (usually very high close to 1).
Masks have the ability to lower the rate of disease transmission in public places. The effectiveness of masks is determined by the user.
### Disease Progression
At the start of the circle, everyone is **"susceptible"**. If a resident becomes infected, she enters her "incubation period" or the "latent" stage. She gets randomly assigned the number of days for her incubation period and her symptomatic period, according to a normal distribution defined by the user. She also gets assigned whether her symptoms will be **""mild"**, **"severe"**, or neither, according to certain probabilities according to the user.
While a resident or her household member is sick, she may or may not continue with her normal routines, depending on whether she is "careless", "cautious", or neither.
Each day, the model re-calculates days to determine if an infected residents should start to be symptomatic, or exit the disease period. By "existing", most residents "recover". However, if a resident has severe symptoms, there is a probability of her dying, determined by the user.
After a resident recovers, she enters a period of immunity, the length and standard deviation of which are determined by the user. During immunity, the residents still may get infected, but the likelihood of getting infected is lowered, controlled by the `how-immune` variable set by the user. The decrease rate of infection of one-time exposures are calculated based on `how-immune` and `immune-days`.
## HOW TO USE IT
To use the model, the user may choose to tune each input on the user interface, setting params to set up the town, make the demographics, and define the disease.
After setting the parameters, users can hit "Reset" to build out the town. Then, users may hit the "Start / Stop Simulation" to start or pause the simulation. Each "tick" in the model represents one day.
In the grid visualization, the "flag" symbols represent schools, and "computer" symbols represent workplaces. The dots represent workers, and arrows represent students or non-workers. The gray color represents susceptibility or immunity. The yellow color represents the incubation period, whereas the red color indicates that a resident is symptomatic.
Three monitors left of the grid show the real-time population, total cases, and total deaths since day 1. On the right, three plots show the daily new cases, new deaths, and active severe cases by day. These metrics are what people are most interested in during a pandemic.
## THINGS TO NOTICE
We noticed a few emergent behaviors in the model:
- Infections typically reach an acute peak and then decline within the first few weeks of "patient zero". Different demographical and behaviral settings may lead to slightly different intensity or duration of this peak, but such an acute peak occurs in most cases.
- After the first peak, subsequent peaks (if any) are usually much less acute.
- The patterns of subsequent infections peaks are highly dependent on *immunity* characteristics.
- Immunity: If immunity is absolute within a certain period, latter peaks are usually less acute, or even non-existent.
- Immune asynchrony: The deviation of immunity duration among people is very important. If people are "synchronous" in their immunity, later peaks are usually more acute, because the a higher ratio of population will reach susceptibility after recovery at the same time. Conversely, if people are "asynchronous" in their immunity, later peaks are usually less acute, and under some circumstances we will observe the second peak being dragged out forever.
- Under circumstances where immunity is not absolute and immunity duration is asynchronous, the disease is not likely to be eradicated, but we will observe cycles of infection peaks and valleys, although later peaks are usually slightly less acute than their predecessor.
## THINGS TO TRY
Users are encouraged to test out different demographical, behavioral, and virological settings to see how they affect the spread of the disease. Meanwhile, users are encouraged to validate and extend the model.
- This model provides a framework where residents performing daily routines while infecting each other, and may refrain from performing routines when getting sick. This framework may be easily extended to simulate more complicated situations, e.g., more diverse routine choices, vaccination, government-enforced lockdowns, etc.
- Users may validate the model after extending it, and then move back and forth between model validation and extension, until they are satisfied with the model's ability to simulate the real-world spread of the disease. Then, the model becomes a useful tool to simulate credible scenarios and for policy makers to make informed decisions.
